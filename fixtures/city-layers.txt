The city wakes up in layers.

First the buses start to hiss at the curb. Then the corner stores roll up their metal gates. A little later, someone drags a chair onto a fire escape, and the day begins to sound like itself.

By eight in the morning, the sidewalks have agreed on a rhythm. Delivery workers cut diagonally through the crowd. Cyclists squeeze through narrow seams in traffic. A dog refuses to cross the street for reasons known only to the dog. Nobody stops for long, but everyone leaves a trace.

At lunch, the park fills with temporary lives. People eat standing up. Someone reads three pages of a paperback and then stares into the middle distance as if the book has made a demand. Two engineers argue quietly about whether a difficult system should be made simpler or merely made more reliable. They do not settle it, but they do finish their sandwiches.

By late afternoon the light gets architectural. Windows stop being transparent and become mirrors. Buildings that looked blunt at noon suddenly have edges again. The whole block feels edited for contrast.

At night the city contracts without becoming still. The restaurants brighten. The offices dim. The same streets carry different intentions. What looked like pressure in the morning starts to read as momentum instead.

From far enough away, it all seems continuous. Up close, it is obviously made of segments.
